INTRODUCTION

Contemplation is the second stage of the Suluk — the spiritual journey of the Inayatiyya. Where Concentration gathered the scattered beam of attention into a single point, Contemplation turns that single point toward the Divine and holds it there. It is the long, patient gaze of the heart upon the One Reality, until the veil between the seer and the Seen becomes thin enough to let the light of that Reality shine through.

This handbook is organized in two parts, one for each retreat weekend of Contemplation Section I. All ten classes (CT1–CT10) were taught by Pir Zia Inayat-Khan:

Part I — The Spring Retreat (CT1–CT6, March 27–29, 2026) opens the Contemplation year with the foundational prayers, purification breaths, and the first movements into mushahada — the witnessing gaze that is the keynote of contemplation.

Part II — The Easter Retreat (CT7–CT10, April 18–19, 2026) deepens the arc with extended Fikr and Zikr, contemplation on the Divine Names, and the subtler practices through which the heart learns to rest in the Presence.

The handbook contains 226 teachings extracted from class transcripts, organized into 18 topic chapters across the two retreats. Within each retreat, chapters are organized by topic — Prayers, Breath, Fikr and Zikr, Wazifa, Contemplation Practices, Teachings, Stories, and more — so you can always find a teaching by subject while keeping the temporal arc of each retreat intact.

"Contemplation is the gaze of the heart upon the One Reality."

— Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

May this handbook serve as a living reference — not a museum of words, but a doorway back into the practices and states from which they arose. Return to it often. The teachings deepen with every reading.

The Practice of Ittisal: Conjunction with Universal Being

CT2 · March 27, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

The practice of Ittisal is an awakening to our inherent rapport with the vaster body, mind, and soul of existence.

The Universal Being

"Ittisal is a word that means conjunction. And specifically, what we have in mind is conjunction with the universal being, which is to say the being of the universe."

The universe has both inner and outer dimensions, which Murshid speaks of as the "spirit of guidance."

"The physical cosmos, you could say, is the body of the universal being."

"Not only are our bodies part of a vaster body, which is the universal body, also our minds are a portion of the universal mind... And not only of consciousness, but of pure intelligence. So beyond the mind of the universe, there is a soul of the universe."

Breathing with the Universe

When we connect to this total being, we are no longer limited to our personal senses. Our individual mind is embedded within a larger mind.

"When we tap into this larger body, mind, soul, we are nourished, we are guided, we are inspired. We're shown things that our personal senses don't perceive."

"The more that we breathe with the universe as a cell within that larger body, the more that we become connected with life itself, the more we are guided and and the more that we're able to contribute to the the coherence of the anatomy that is unfolding itself around us."

Energizing Ittisal with the Intonation of 'Ha'

CT2 · March 27, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

Ittisal—conjunction—is a practice of awakening our rapport with the vaster body, mind, and soul of the universe. This realization can be energized in the body through a series of intonations, the first of which is the syllable "Ha."

The Practice

  1. Intone the syllable "Ha."
  2. "The way that Ha works is that it... rises up from the coccyx, from the the tailbone, right up into the chest. And you feel its culmination in in the chest."
  3. Feel through the sound how your physical and subtle bodies are embedded within the universe "as a kind of eddy, a whirlpool of the cosmic matter."

The Realization: Little Body, Great Body

This practice cultivates a profound shift in perspective. We are a vortex of the cosmic substance, constantly exchanging matter and energy with the whole.

"You feel that... when you sense that this physical body is your little body, and that you also have a great body, which is equally yours."

"Normally we think, well, this body is mine and I live in the universe. But you could equally say, this is my little body and the universe is my great body."

The Vigilant Practice of Muraqaba

CT3 · March 28, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

The practice of contemplation, or Muraqaba, is deeply connected to a divine name.

"Muraqabah is related to the word Raqib, which is a name of God, the vigilant, the watchful."

"When we practice Muraqabah, we are participating in this divine quality. It's a quality that already pervades existence. Attentive, watchful care."

This is an invitation to shift from a state of sleepwalking to one of profound wakefulness. The practice asks:

"What would it be like not merely to stumble through life, not merely to sleepwalk, but to take every step wakefully, vigilantly?"

The Contemplation of Respect (I'tibar)

Another word for contemplation is I'tibar, which also means respect. This connection is key to the practice.

"Contemplation means that we don't take anything for granted. We see deeply into everyone and everything. We recognize its source."

It is a commitment to engage with the world with a new level of seriousness and presence, recognizing the divine signs in all that we encounter.

"Nothing is just an object, nothing is just a reflective surface. Everything has depth, everything has infinite depth. And there is a meaning, in fact a host of meanings to be discovered in everything we encounter. Nothing is negligible."

I'tibar: Contemplation as an Act of Respect

CT3 · March 28, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

Contemplation is Respect

"There's another word that also refers to contemplation, I'tibar... it also means respect--paying respect to something is I'tibar, the same word which means contemplation."

"So contemplation means that we don't take anything for granted. We see deeply into everyone and everything. We recognize its source. Nothing is just an object, nothing is just a reflective surface. Everything has depth, everything has infinite depth."

Crossing Over into Depth

The meaning of I'tibar is deepened by its linguistic root, 'abara, which implies a movement from the surface into the heart of a thing.

"The word is related to 'abara, which means to cross, like to cross a river, to wade across a river or even to swim across. Crossing over, wading through."

"Whatever you meet with in waking life or in a dream, you wouldn't just meet it as something confronting you. You wouldn't only regard its surface. You'd find a way into it, through it."

This crossing-over transforms our relationship with the world. "There's a porousness to all things and in I'tibar we become porous, we become vaporous, we move through each other. Objects become subjects, subjects not only meet but pass through each other, wade into each other."

The Emergence of Meaning

This respectful passage into depth is how we discover the inner significance, or 'ibara--"meaning"--which comes from the same root.

"Meaning comes when there's respect, there is a movement through and across into the depth, and there is an interpretation... we don't only see the obvious in everything, we adduce meaning that is implied, that's evoked."

"In the encounter there's a movement of spirit toward the depth, from the outer evidence to the inner meaning to the name, the divine name."

Ta'bir: The Interpretation of Dreams

CT3 · March 28, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

"A related word is Ta'bir. It comes from the same root [as I'tibar] and it means interpretation, for instance the interpretation of dreams."

"You might see something in a dream and when you wake up, when you wake, you just jump out of bed and completely forget what happened in the night."

The Contemplative Approach

To practice Ta'bir, or dream interpretation, is to be watchful and vigilant upon waking.

"If you're watchful, if you're vigilant, you'll slowly emerge out of sleep and recollect everything that you experienced, as much as you can recall."

"And if you have I'tibar, then you not only remember there was this incident and this person appeared, but you sense behind the narrative a meaning, a signification, things are hinted at that you are meant to know, but you have to linger over it."

A Personal Knowledge

While dream dictionaries exist, the meaning of a dream is ultimately a personal experience that cannot be found externally.

"One symbol doesn't mean the same thing for every dreamer. Yes, we share cultures and so symbols have similar resonances, but the interpretation of a dream is always going to be a very personal experience."

"And someone could help you to find the meaning of the dream, but really they're just helping you to identify the knowledge that is already your own."

While dream dictionaries exist, the meaning of a symbol is never fixed. It is a deeply personal and contextual experience, rooted in the individual.

"Ibn Sirin wrote a dream dictionary, but one symbol doesn't mean the same thing for every dreamer. Yes, we share cultures and so symbols have similar resonances, but the interpretation of a dream is always going to be a very personal experience."

The Spiritual Rhythm of Opening, Bestowal, and Patience

CT4 · March 28, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

"These three divine names form a sequence that describes a profound spiritual process. Ya Fattah is the Opener, the one who removes obstacles and opens the way. Ya Wahhab is the Bestower, the Giver of gifts, the one who bestows grace without measure. And Ya Sabur is the Patient, the one who is steadfast and endures."

The Foundation: Ya Sabur (The Patient)

The foundation of this entire process is patience. "We must cultivate patience as we wait for the opening and the subsequent bestowal."

This is not a passive waiting, but an active and intentional state of being.

"This is not a passive or resigned waiting, but an active, vibrant steadfastness. It is the quality of endurance, of holding firm in our intention and our practice, even when we see no immediate results. In practicing with Ya Sabur, we align ourselves with the timeless, patient nature of the Divine itself."

The Prayer: Ya Fattah (The Opener)

"When we call upon Ya Fattah, we are acknowledging that the opening we seek is not something we can engineer through our own will."

When we feel constricted or blocked, this is a prayer for divine intervention.

"It is like a flower bud that cannot force itself to bloom; it must wait for the sun to coax its petals open. We turn to the Opener, trusting that in the right time, the way will be made clear."

The Grace: Ya Wahhab (The Bestower)

"Following the opening is the bestowal, Ya Wahhab. Once the channel is cleared by Fattah, the grace of Wahhab can flow."

This bestowal is a gift that is both precisely what is needed and entirely unearned.

"This is the divine gift, the Fazl of God, which is unearned and unconditional. It is the answer to the heart's deepest longing, the nourishment that the soul requires for its journey."

The Rhythm of Spiritual Life

The sequence is one of profound trust and surrender.

"We hold our ground with patience, Ya Sabur. We pray for an opening from the Opener, Ya Fattah. And we open our hands and our hearts to receive the gift from the Bestower, Ya Wahhab."

"This is the rhythm of the spiritual life: a patient waiting, a sudden opening, and a generous outpouring of grace. It is a process that unfolds according to a wisdom that is beyond our comprehension."

The Swing of Breath: Rising Above Birth and Death

CT5 · March 29, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

A foundational practice of Fikr, or remembrance, that uses the natural rhythm of the breath as a vehicle for spiritual ascent. The process begins by simply watching the breath as it moves.

"As the breath goes out far and comes in deep, the effect is that of a swing, going out, falling back, going out, falling back."

The Practice

"And in Fikr we place a phrase on the swing."

  1. On the Inhale, inwardly say: "I rise above birth and death."
  2. On the Exhale, inwardly say: "through the waves of the air."
  3. Continue this cycle for several breaths.
  4. "We not only inwardly say these words, we live them." Feel the sensation of floating, gliding, and soaring.

Integration

After some time, "let the words fade away, but stay in the experience of floating on waves, rising above birth and death."

The practice culminates in a state of dual awareness—maintaining the experience of being beyond birth and death while simultaneously remaining grounded and present.

"Let's see if you can be on two levels at once, beyond birth and death, but also right here, right now, in the room, your feet on the ground."

The Spiritual Marriage of Opposites

CT6 · March 29, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

Beyond the False Dichotomy

We must be careful not to fall into a false dichotomy where light is good and darkness is something to be shunned or overcome.

"Whenever we meet with a polarity in creation and we... affirm only one pole, there's a danger of one-sidedness."

"In fact, the opposite poles have something of each other in each and they meet in a spiritual marriage. The coincidentia oppositorum."

"The great Sufi al-Kharraz said to look for God precisely where the opposites meet."

Darkness Reveals Light

Darkness is not merely the absence of light. It is the condition that allows subtler light to be seen--like the stars, which are invisible in the blue sky but brilliant in the blackness of night.

"The veiling of the light of the sun enables other manifestations of light to be revealed."

"Instead of thinking of darkness as the opposite of light, one begins to discover modalities of light, gradations of light, frequencies of light. And one finds that by filtering out the most obvious and most glaring frequencies, the result is not the dispelling of luminosity, but the discovery of of finer transmissions of light."

The Midnight Sun

"This is why Sufis speak of the midnight sun, the sun that can only be beheld in the middle of the night, a black sun. The luminous manifestations that emerge in the shade."

"Just as there are things that are easier for our mind to see when our physical eyes are closed... to attain the inner vision, it's necessary to close one's eyes."

From Earth to Water: Concentration to Contemplation

CT1 · March 27, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

The four stages of spiritual practice can be compared to the four elements. The journey begins with Earth, the work of concentration, and now turns toward Water, the practice of contemplation.

The Earth Element -- Concentration (Mujahada)

The first stage of practice corresponds to the heavy, dense, and slow qualities of the Earth element.

"Earth, as you know, is very heavy, very dense, very slow. It corresponds to concentration."

"In our fast-moving world where attention is so rare, it's the first medicine. The very first thing to learn is to slow down and be like a rock. Just learn to be fully still, fully silent, fully present."

A stone carries an abiding vibration because its consciousness is not scattered. "It's very rooted, very stable. And so this is what we've been developing in concentration."

The Water Element -- Contemplation (Muraqabah)

After establishing the stillness and stability of Earth, we transition to the element of Water. This is the turn from concentration to contemplation.

"After the Earth element comes the element of water. This is the shift we've reached now as we enter into contemplation. So having been still, having been focused, focused on one object, now we're going to begin to move. The mind is going to begin to move."

This is not the random, reactive movement of an untrained mind.

"We didn't want the mind to move until we were present and focused because it would just move randomly, it would just react to stimuli. But now that we've drawn our attention in and stabilized it and deepened it, now we can let it unfold, let it find its way."

This new movement is purposeful and artful, exploring the subtler dimensions of reality.

"This is what contemplation does... for the mind to move, but not erratically, not randomly, but gracefully, artfully, purposefully. This is Muraqabah."

The Practice of Gazing into Darkness

"Sufis speak of the midnight sun, the sun that can only be beheld in the middle of the night, a black sun. The luminous manifestations that emerge in the shade."

"Instead of thinking of darkness as the opposite of light, one begins to discover modalities of light, gradations of light, frequencies of light."

"By filtering out the most obvious and most glaring frequencies, the result is not the dispelling of luminosity, but the discovery of of finer transmissions of light."

The Practice

A practice of pure witnessing, allowing the eye to perceive the light inherent in total darkness.

  1. "Find the darkest space that you can find and darken it as fully as possible." You might go into a closet or bathroom with no windows, placing a towel under the door so no light comes in.
  2. "Sit in the darkness with open eyes and as much as possible without blinking. Not painfully so, but try to avoid blinking as much as possible."
  3. "Look into the darkness and see what you see."
  4. Continue for about ten minutes or more.

"We're not projecting any kind of image, whether preconceived or spontaneously imagined. We're not imagining. We're just witnessing the light that appears to your eyes in the darkness."

"That sense of spatial distance dissolves in the darkness and the vision might be happening in your retina, in your brain, in a disembodied mind. It doesn't matter. You're just meeting the vision of the light in the darkness."

"That this name has come down through revelation, has been passed down through the silsila, and now is given to you as a trust to, yes, to say, say it with your tongue, certainly, but more than that, to enter its field. When you have a Wazifa for that time, you enter the field of that name. You dedicate your life to beholding God through the portal of this particular name."

"A door is open to you. And as soon as that Wazifa is given and accepted, yes, you say the name diligently each day in the morning, or with some Wazifas it will be in the evening. But more than that, you live your life in the continuous presence of that divine attribute. You become the 'abd, you become the servant of that name."

Murshid's Teaching — Read in Full by Pir Zia

"Every spoken word is a re-echo of thought and every thought is the re-echo of an emotion. And as the activity from within manifests on the surface, so the word spoken takes its reverse process. It is reproduced on the mind, and from the mind, it is reproduced on the heart, the factor of feeling. That which comes from within comes involuntarily, and what enters within from without enters voluntarily."

"Therefore, Wazifa is taught by Sufis as a voluntary production of a certain thing on the mind and in the heart. Mind being powerful to produce that which is impressed upon it from within and without. As many times as a word is repeated, even in a whisper, so many records it has produced in one's mind and in the heart."

Principles of Practice (from Murshid)
PrincipleGuidance
**Numbers**Must be in accordance with the law of numbers — set count, regular rhythm; without consideration of numbers, rhythm and balance are lost
**Moon phase**Commence during the rise of the moon (progressive trend); waning moon brings upset and confusion
**Place**Same place every day creates a growing atmosphere
**Time**Same time every day — invisible forces get accustomed to meet and help; without a set time, it becomes difficult to gather them
**Purity**Cleanliness of body, purity of mind, fragrance, beautiful thoughts give quicker result
**Diet**Flesh food is sometimes undesirable for Wazifa; disturbances of mind make it fruitless
**Character**Weakness of character and an impious life ruins the life of the one who repeats Wazifa
**Secrecy**Keep the Wazifa secret and its effect secret; pride of repeating should be avoided
**Authorization**Must be taught by a blessed soul or permission taken from a holy person — produces a thousandfold effect

The Story (from the Mahabharata)

Pir Zia told this story to illuminate the chivalric rule about sacred responsibility:

"An example would be a story that is told at the culmination of the Mahabharata, great Hindu epic, which has as its heroes, Yudhisthira and his wife Draupadi and his brothers. Now, they go through many ordeals in the course of a great conflict that occurs. And finally emerge victorious."

"But the story doesn't end there. Having concluded their earthly affairs satisfactorily, this family resolves to embark on a pilgrimage into the mountains with a view to ascending Mount Meru and retiring there in a state of samadhi, meditative absorption."

"Along the way, a dog, black dog, arrives and joins them, and they enjoy the dog's companionship. But tragically, in the course of the ordeals and vicissitudes of the venture, one after the other, Yudhisthira's brothers — they perish. They lose their lives one after another. And Draupadi does also. And so Yudhisthira has the solemn duty of cremating them. And in the end, it's only Yudhisthira and the dog."

"And the mountain top is looming in the distance, but Yudhisthira is nearly exhausted and the remaining span seems almost impossible to traverse. And it's just then that a flying car comes down from the sky. And the angel who is steering it says, step into the car and come with me and I'll take you to your destination. Only the dog may not come."

Al-Hayy — The Living (First Mother Name)

CT1 · March 27, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat Khan

Al-Hayy is the first of the seven Ummahat (mother names). Pir Zia introduced it with a teaching on mantric language, why livingness is the first attribute, and what it means to discover the ground of life within.

Introduction

"Now we are going to work with the Ummahat, the mother names. And the first of these is Al-Hayy. Al-Hayy. It's an aspirated H, Al-Hayy, Al-Hayy meaning the living."

Mantric Language — The Sound Embodies the Meaning

"Now as soon as you hear the name Al-Hayy, you will recognize how it exemplifies the reality of what we could call mantric language, which is to say words, names that in their very sonic structure embody the quality they designate."

"Even if you are not told that Hayy means the living, if you would simply recite Hayy, Hayy, Hayy, Hayy, you would find in your breath a surge of livingness that confirms the meaning of the word. It's in the sound itself."

Why Al-Hayy Is First

"So this is the first of the seven, Al-Hayy. Why is it the first? Before something can be something, certainly before something can do something, something must simply be. Something must simply exist, live. This comes first. Being, living."

Life Beyond Physical Embodiment

"All too often when we use the word living, we think living means being in a body with a beating heart. And when the body dies, then life ends. But really, embodied life is only one mode of life. There's life in the plain of the subtle physical world. There's life on the plain of the mental realm. There's life on the plain of the angelic intelligences."

"Life encompasses all planes. So it is an error to identify life exclusively with physical embodiment. Life is something more innate. Long before we incarnated, already we lived. After this body dissolves into its elements, still we'll live. In fact, life never dies. Death dies. Life lives."

"Life is innate, perpetual, primary. And the universe begins with it. Creation unfolds from it. Our own individual journey has this inception. Pure, sheer life."

The Ground of Livingness

"You could ask yourself, how does it feel to be alive? And the first answer might be, it's good to look out through these eyes, it's good to breathe fresh air, it's good to be alive. Well, this is true, but it isn't the end of the story. If you look deeper, it isn't only this eyesight that evidences your livingness."

"You can sink down into yourself and discover a ground of life that is more ancient than the novel manifestations of being that we identify so exclusively with. And still you can say, it's good to be alive."

"There's a felicity in the sheer livingness of the soul. Our livingness in the one that is inalienable. Yes, our mind might become oblivious to the innateness of it, but that innateness persists. Life is intrinsic, and it marks divinity. It belongs to God."

"This is divine nature in its primal mode before all of the names that describe the unfolding relationship between the whole and the parts. Sheer livingness."

The Copper Rule of Willing Help

CT8 · April 18, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

The Rule

"My conscientious self, extend your help willingly to those in need."

The rules always begin with the phrase "My conscientious self," which is a crucial reminder of their purpose.

"It means that we're speaking to ourselves. We're not, um, pointing a finger at anyone else, and we're not going to use these rules to admonish anyone. Everyone is responsible for themself and accountable to their own conscience."

"You can imagine a world in which when people found themselves in real need, others recognized the fact and were there to help. It could happen to any of us at any time."

"If we're all committed to the principle, we can all find our way through together. But if everyone is out for themself, in the end, everyone loses."

The Story of the Four Friends

A Jataka tale illustrates this principle. There were four friends living in a forest: a hare, a jackal, an otter, and a monkey. The hare proposed that on the next holy day, they should fast and offer whatever food they found to someone who might be hungry. The friends all agreed.

The next morning, the jackal found a jar of milk and a piece of meat. The otter found fish buried in the sand. The monkey plucked ripe mangoes from a tree. Each of them, while willing to give their findings away, secretly hoped they would get to keep the meal for themselves.

The hare, however, was in a state of apprehension. "I eat grass, but no one else eats grass." What could he possibly offer?

Meanwhile, a fairy, the guardian spirit of the forest, had overheard their pledge and decided to test their resolve. Disguised as an emaciated old woman, she approached each of them in turn. The jackal, the otter, and the monkey all willingly offered up their food when she asked, but each time, she declined and moved on.

Finally, she came to the hare, who had nothing to give. He was utterly distraught until an idea flashed in his mind.

"I have nothing to give, but let me just give myself."

He asked the old woman if they could make a fire. She caused a fire to leap up.

"And the hare smiled and without any hesitation, leaped into the fire to be cooked, to be offered as food for the crone."

But something strange occurred--the flames were cool. The fairy revealed her true form and said, "Oh, hare, I never meant to eat you. I was just playing a game, but your heart is so pure, so generous, so kind."

Then the fairy drew energy from a nearby hill, lifted her wand to the moon, and inscribed the hare's portrait upon it.

"And that's why when you go out to do your Shughl-i-Mahtabi at night on a clear night and you look up at the moon, you just might see a hare gazing down at you."

The Meaning

"A story like this can say many things. Of course, it's a celebration of generosity and particularly the willingness to help someone in their hour of need, even if it means personal sacrifice."

Using the Contemplative Walk for Guidance

CT9 · April 19, 2026 · Pir Zia Inayat-Khan

When facing dilemmas or hardships, our first response is often reactive. "Very often the response is something that comes from conditioning, it comes from fearfulness." It's tempting to act immediately, but if we can learn to pause, we find there is always a better way.

"The action won't come well if it comes from sheer exasperation." The contemplative walk offers a method to move beyond reactivity and open to a wiser course of action.

The Practice for Receiving Guidance

  1. Consider the Situation

Before you walk, take a moment to hold the problem in your mind. "Think about it, think about the different sides, who you think is right, who you think is wrong, what all of the evidence is from every point of view... Take a moment, just let that all be there in your mind."

  1. Put It All Aside

Once you have considered the matter, consciously release it. "And then put it all aside. Put it completely aside."

  1. Walk with Presence

During the walk, your focus is not on the problem. "You're not thinking about the situation. But you're also not repressing it, you're not running away from it... on the walk, you're just feeling every footstep... breathing the fresh air, you're airing out your heart, you're letting your heart breathe."

  1. Listen for the Insight

After walking for some time, an insight may arise naturally, not from effortful analysis but from a state of calm openness.

The Nature of the Insight

This process creates the space for a solution to appear. "It didn't come from thinking hard about the problem. It was just that after you cleared the air and breathed freely, you were shown what you need to do."

This applies to intense conflicts as well as life's crossroads. It complements, rather than replaces, rational thought. "You do your research, you think hard, but then you put it all aside and just breathe and walk and open yourself to guidance. And what comes then, that's what you listen to."

The guidance that emerges is distinct from a reactive impulse. "It's not something that you're compelled to do by the intensity of your emotional reaction... it's just what guidance is showing you now that you've calmly put everything aside and walked through a landscape in the presence of God."
